Understanding Heart Attack Risk Factors

Age

As age increases, so does the risk of heart disease. About 80% of people who die of coronary heart disease are 65 or older.

Blood Pressure (trestbps)

High blood pressure can damage arteries that feed your heart by making them less elastic, decreasing blood and oxygen flow to your heart. Normal range is typically below 120/80 mmHg.

Cholesterol (chol)

High levels of cholesterol can increase your risk of heart disease. LDL (bad) cholesterol contributes to plaque buildup in arteries, while HDL (good) cholesterol helps remove LDL from the bloodstream.

Maximum Heart Rate (thalach)

Maximum heart rate achieved during exercise testing. A lower-than-expected maximum heart rate during peak exercise can indicate heart problems.

ST Depression (oldpeak)

ST depression induced by exercise relative to rest indicates reduced blood flow to the heart (ischemia). Higher values suggest increased risk.

Sex

Men generally have a higher risk of heart disease. However, after menopause, women's risk increases to match men's risk.

Chest Pain Type (cp)

Typical angina: Classic chest pain triggered by physical or emotional stress.
Atypical angina: Chest pain not fitting typical patterns.
Non-anginal pain: Chest pain unrelated to heart.
Asymptomatic: No chest pain symptoms despite underlying heart issues.

Fasting Blood Sugar (fbs)

Elevated blood sugar (>120 mg/dl) can indicate diabetes, which significantly increases heart disease risk by damaging blood vessels and nerves that control the heart.

Resting ECG Results (restecg)

Electrocardiogram measurements taken at rest can reveal heart abnormalities even when you're not experiencing symptoms.

Exercise-Induced Angina (exang)

Chest pain during physical activity suggests that your heart muscle isn't getting enough blood flow, indicating a higher risk of coronary artery disease.

Slope of ST Segment (slope)

The shape of the ST segment on an ECG during exercise. Downsloping patterns are typically associated with higher risk compared to upsloping or flat patterns.

Number of Major Vessels (ca)

The number of major blood vessels (0-4) with significant narrowing as revealed by fluoroscopy. More affected vessels indicate more extensive heart disease.

Thalassemia (thal)

A blood disorder that affects the body's ability to produce hemoglobin. Certain types can impact heart health and increase the risk of coronary artery disease.

Preventive Measures for Heart Health

Physical Activity

Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week to help maintain heart health.

Healthy Diet

Focus on fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and limit saturated fats, sodium, and added sugars.

Regular Check-ups

Monitor your blood pressure, cholesterol, and blood sugar levels regularly through medical check-ups.
