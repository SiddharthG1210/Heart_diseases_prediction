Understanding Heart Disease Risk Factors

Disclaimer: This tool provides a risk assessment based on medical data patterns but is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Consult with a healthcare provider for proper diagnosis and treatment.

Age

Age is a significant non-modifiable risk factor. The risk of heart disease increases substantially with age, particularly after 45 for men and 55 for women.

Higher risk: Above 45 (men), Above 55 (women)

Sex

Men generally have a higher risk of heart disease than premenopausal women. After menopause, women's risk increases to match men's.

Higher risk: Male, Postmenopausal female

Chest Pain Type

The type of chest pain is crucial for diagnosis. Typical angina (pain caused by reduced blood flow to the heart) is a strong indicator of coronary heart disease.

Higher risk: Typical angina, Asymptomatic with underlying issues

Resting Blood Pressure

Persistent high blood pressure damages arteries and accelerates atherosclerosis, increasing the risk of heart attacks and strokes.

Normal range: Below 120/80 mmHg
Higher risk: Above 140/90 mmHg

Cholesterol

High cholesterol contributes to plaque buildup in arteries. LDL (bad) cholesterol narrows arteries, while HDL (good) cholesterol helps remove LDL.

Normal range: Below 200 mg/dL
Higher risk: Above 240 mg/dL

Fasting Blood Sugar

Elevated blood sugar levels can damage blood vessels and the nerves that control the heart. Diabetes significantly increases heart disease risk.

Normal range: Below 100 mg/dL
Higher risk: Above 126 mg/dL (Diabetes)

Resting ECG

Electrocardiogram results can reveal abnormalities in heart rhythm, blood supply, or heart muscle damage, which may indicate heart disease even without symptoms.

Higher risk: ST-T Wave abnormality, Left ventricular hypertrophy

Max Heart Rate

Maximum heart rate achieved during exercise can indicate cardiovascular fitness. A lower than expected max heart rate for age may suggest heart problems.

Normal range: ~220 - age
Higher risk: Significantly lower than age-predicted maximum

Exercise Induced Angina

Chest pain triggered by physical exertion is a warning sign that the heart muscle isn't getting enough blood during increased demand.

Higher risk: Presence of exercise-induced angina

ST Depression (Oldpeak)

ST segment depression on an ECG during exercise indicates reduced blood flow to the heart. Greater depression suggests more severe ischemia.

Normal range: Near 0 mm
Higher risk: >2 mm depression

Slope of ST Segment

The slope of the ST segment during an exercise stress test provides information about the type and severity of cardiac ischemia.

Higher risk: Flat or downsloping ST segment

Number of Major Vessels

The number of major coronary arteries with significant blockage (>50%) as revealed by fluoroscopy. More blocked vessels indicate more extensive heart disease.

Higher risk: Greater number of vessels with significant blockage

Thalassemia

Thalassemia is a blood disorder that can affect the heart. In cardiac context, it refers to blood flow abnormalities observed during nuclear stress testing.

Higher risk: Reversible defect (suggests ischemia)
